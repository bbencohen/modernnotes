The SCAP

  • MacArthur was named the Supreme Commander of the Allied Powers (SCAP) or Supreme Commander of the Occupation forces, felt that only a democratic Japan would be truly peace-loving
  • American government believed that establishing democracy in Japan involved change in all areas of Japanese life - tremendous changes in just seven short years
  • The “Gentle Conqueror”

Phase 1: 1945-1947

  • Fundamental changes for the Japanese Government and society
  • Allies punished Japan for its past militarism and expansion by convening war crimes trials in Tokyo
  • MacArthur dismantled the Japanese Army and banned former military officers from taking roles of political leadership in the new government
  • Introduction of land reform, designed to benefit the majority of tenant farmers and reduce the power of rich landowners, many of whom had advocated for war and supported Japanese expansionism in the 1930s
  • Break up the large zaibatsu to transform the economy into a free market capitalist system
  • Developing a new constitution (1947), downgrading the emperor’s status to a figurehead, placing more power in the parliamentary system, promoting greater rights and privileges for women, and renouncing the right to wage war, which involved eliminating all non-defensive armed forces
  • Repatriation of Japanese soldiers

Reform Militaristic Culture

  • Private business
    • Break up the zaibatsu, remove the monopoly that companies (i.e. Mitsubishi) had over the whole economy
  • Economy
  • Armed forces

Phase 2: 1947-48

  • An economic crisis in Japan & concerns about the spread of communism sparked a reconsideration of occupation policies
  • The economic rehabilitation of Japan took center stage
  • SCAP became concerned that a weak Japanese economy would increase the influence of the domestic communist movement
  • Communist victory in China’s civil war put the future of East Asia appeared at risk, Japan ‘needed’ US values
  • Forced tax reforms to measure aimed at conrtolling inflation
  • Shortage of raw materials required to feed Japanese industries and markets for finished goods were addressed with the outbreak of the Korean War
  • Japan became the principal supply depot for UN forces
  • Korean War placed Japan firmly within the confines of the US defense perimeter in Asia, assuring the Japanese protection

Phase 3: 1950 (diplomacy)

  • SCAP deemed the political and economic future of Japan firmly established and set about securing a formal peace treaty to end both the war and the occupation
  • The idea of a rearmed and militant Japan no longer alarmed US officials, the real threat was communism, particularly in Asia.
  • The final agreement allowed the USA to maintain their bases in Okinawa and elsewhere in Japan, and the US Government promised Japan a bilateral security pact
  • In September of 1951, fifty-two nation met in San Francisco to discuss the treaty, and ultimately 49 of them signed it. Notable holdouts included the USSR, Poland, and Czechoslovakia all of which objected to the promise to support the Republic of China and not do business with the PRC that was forced on Japan by US Politicians.

On the 2nd of September 1945 the Japanese formally surrendered on the battle ship Missouri. General MacArthur was named Supreme Commander for the Allied powers (SCAP). Although UK, USSR, and ROC had an advisory role as part of an “Allied Council,” MacArthur had the final authority to make all decisions The occupation of Japan can be divided into three phases: the initial effort to punish and reform Japan, the work to revive the Japanese economy, and the conclusion of a formal peace treaty and alliance.

Macarthur was immediately faced with an enormous problem of starvation, disease and housing as winter approached. MacArthur ordered 3.5 million tonnes of food. Within 6 months, food supplies stored around the Pacific and South East Asia for potential American invasion of Japan began to arrive. Under the Potsdam Declaration the Post-War Japanese government was to come from the free will of the people. MacArthur wanted to hold elections in early 1946 and therefore needed a new constitution before then. The political leaders of Japan wanted to ensure that (while the militarists would be removed) Japan would be governed conservatively. The Japanese politicians moved too slowly, so MacArthur wrote his own new constitution. The occupation force, at its height, was dominated 500,000 Americans. The American Occupation of Japan was premised on the notion that the thorough demilitarization and democratization of the defeated nation would make the world forever safe from the renewed threat of Japanese aggression. The drafting of a new, democratic constitution was considered essential to Japan’s recasting as a peaceful member of the community of nations. After a Japanese commission failed to produce a new national constitution sufficiently progressive for the Occupation’s liking, a document was drafted (over the span of only a week’s time) in-house by American staff and presented to the Japanese government for translation and enactment. The Japanese had no choice but to follow orders and the new constitution, somewhat awkwardly worded as the result of its English-language origins, was promulgated in November 1946 and came into effect on May 3, 1947. Many scholars have noted the irony of the Occupation installing democratic political institutions in Japan through transparently authoritarian means: the Japanese, it has been said, were “forced to be free” by their American occupiers. Nonetheless, the 1947 Constitution was readily embraced by the Japanese people and has endured (with not a single amendment over the past six decades) as a sound basis for Japan’s postwar democracy.
