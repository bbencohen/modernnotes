Battle of the Coral Sea (May 5-8, 1942)

Tactical Victory - Japanese

  • Tactical victory for the Japanese, as they managed to
    • sink the heavy carrier USS Lexington
    • heavily damage the USS Yorktown
    • and sink a destroyed (USS Sims) and an oiler (USS Nesso)
  • Americans managed to achieve the first substantial warship kill against the Japanese Navy by
    • Sinking the light carrier INS Shoho
    • And severely damaged the INS Shokaku
    • and destroying the all aircraft of the carrier INS Zuikaku

Strategic Victory - America and her Allies

  • From a strategic perspective, though, the Americans scored an important, multi-dimensional victory
  1. Invasion of Port Moresby was thwarted
    1. Boosting Allied chances in the bitterly fought New Guinea campaign
    1. Fending off a threat to the supply lines running between the US and Australia
  1. Japanese were denied the services of their two newest carriers on the eve of the Battle of Midway
    1. Shokaku was in the repair shop
    1. Zuikaku spent until June 12 replenishing her aircraft and pilots. Had these two carriers been available on June 4 things might well have turned out very differently at Midway - Japan‚Äôs best pilots of the war were killed at Coral Sea

    Argument: The victory in Midway was somewhat predicated on the strategic victory of the Allies at the Coral Sea

    Japanese success was very much reliant on the war being fast and speedy. It is drawn out for longer than 6 months and that is arguably the point where they inevitably lose as their resources manpower, production speeds, and repair speeds becomes enough to actively thwart their plans

    Turning Point: Impact on the Japanese

    • Long Term Impact
    • The loss of highly trained aircrew from the carriers was never to be made up
    • Prior to the battle of the Coral Sea and (even more so) Midway, Japanese naval aviation was unchallengeable in two oceans. This was the first time Japanese advance had been stopped
    • Long years of hard peace-time training, and real-life exercises against the hapless Chinese (and the scarcely less hapless western Allies) had honed an elite group of Japanese flyers
    • japan could manufacture plenty of replacement aircraft, and at least a few replacement carriers, but could not replace the most skilled naval pilots in the world
    • From this time on, Japanese naval aviation began to decline

    If the Japanese had been successful here, there would have been no Battle of Midway

    • The plan was to take Port Moresby - if the battle of the Coral Sea had been successful they would have been able to take Port Moresby and the Battle of Midway would never have happened.

    Turning Point: Impact on the Americans

    • Lessons Learned: The US Navy learned a great deal from the Battle of the Coral Sea. From the loss of Lexington the Navy learned better ways to contain aviation fuel and control dfensive fighter aircraft
    • From the attacks on the Japanese carriers it learned more about coordination of dive-bombers and torpedo bombers to best effect (too late to help with Midway, but of value longer-term)
    • (Most importantly of all?) it demonstrated that the Japanese could be beaten
    • Damage to Yorktown was estimated to require months in port, a miracle of improvisation she was made and she was made battle worthy after just three days in Pearl Harbour, playing a vital part role at Midway

    HOWEVER

    the loss of Lexington was a severe blow, but in time the US was able to replace ship, aircraft, and trained crew with others superior in all respects.
