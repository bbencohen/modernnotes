"By the end of 1933 Japan had become almost entirely estranged from much of the rest of the world, thanks to the 1931 invasion of Manchuria (and its subsequent re-creation as the puppet state of Manchukuo) and the country’s withdrawal from the League of Nations. However, Tokyo’s greatest fear was the spread of Soviet influence in East Asia, and therefore in November 1936 Japan joined Germany in signing the Anti-Comintern Pact. Given that the United States had considerable trading and missionary interests in China, the Sino-Japanese War could not help but attract attention from Washington. President Franklin Roosevelt wasted no time in branding Japan as an aggressor and enacting a policy that involved a combination of aid to China and economic sanctions against Japan. Moreover, when in December 1937 Japanese aircraft attacked and sank the American gunboat U.S.S. Panay in the Yangtze River, the administration demanded (and received) a formal apology and indemnity from Tokyo Japan’s foreign policy was initially a response to western intimidation however it soon took on its own imperialistic traits that were backed heavily by new found nationalism born in the chaos of the 1920s. It was this nationalism that altered the balance of Japanese foreign policy in the late 30s, democracy and triggered the start of the Pacific War. It was this in conjunction with the instilled military dominance of political Japan and the string of disappointing foreign policy set backs that led to their adoption of more aggressive foreign measures, which can consequently be considered as a primary driving force on the road to the Pacific war."

Hirota Cabinet's National and Foreign Policies (Informally reported to the Emperor on 15 August 1936) Fundamental Principles of National Policy

  • Article 1a:
    • "It is the embodiment of the spirit of the Japanese way of life to attempt to achieve unity in East Asia based on a "live and let live" principle. This may be accomplished by destroying the Great Powers' East Asiatic policy of aggression
    • Greater East Asia Co. (Asia for Asians)
      • Manchuria
      • Indochina
      • Dutch East Indies
  • Article 1b:
    • Communism - Soviets
    • Imperial nations / colonisation
    • Pursuit of resources (oil)
    • Tripartite Pact
    • "National defence" "safeguarding development"
  • Article 1c:
    • Anti-communist anti-Soviet policies
    • Anti-comintern
    • Also mentioning Manchuria, consolidating land ownership there etc
  • Article 1d:
    • Aiming to expand into islands in the South Sea (i.e. Dutch East Indies, Philippines, etc.) via peaceful means if possible
  • Article 2a 1:
    • Militarisation/remilitarisation of Manchuria, Korea. Specifically due to the perceived Communist threat from USSR
  • Article 2a 2:
    • Aiming to strengthen the navy specifically to be on par to the USA Navy, to establish dominance over the Western Pacific
  • Article 2c 1:
    • Consolidation of national ideas, aiming to strengthen national pride and faith in emperor/military
  • Article 2c 2:
    • Establish governmental institutions to promote industries and national policies
  • Article 2c 3:
    • Establishing good living conditions for Japanese people
  • Article 2c 4:
    • Strengthening Japanese air combat ability (plane warfare etc)
  • Article 2c 5:
    • Autarky for Japan, so they don't need to rely on other countries for military capability
  • Article 2c 6:
    • Propaganda strengthened to establish further support throughout Japan etc
